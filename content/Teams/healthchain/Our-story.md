## Our Story HealthChain

### How We Started

HealthChain began with a simple concern noticed across everyday health interactions in Nigerian communities. While engaging with primary healthcare centres, local clinics, and residents across Lagos, Abuja, Kano, and Rivers State, the team encountered repeated confusion around medical history, missing records, and delayed care. What initially appeared as a technology gap quickly revealed itself as a lived daily struggle for families, health workers, and community volunteers who were doing their best within fragile systems. The team was drawn into the problem not by theory, but by witnessing how easily a person’s health story could disappear.

### What We Heard and Observed

Community conversations consistently pointed to the same experiences. Mothers spoke about vaccination cards damaged by floods or misplaced during relocation. Nurses described starting every consultation from zero because no patient history followed the individual. Health volunteers shared frustration about hearing of outbreaks only after sickness had already spread. Clinic administrators expressed that while authorities requested data, they lacked even the most basic tools to store or transmit it.

Walking through clinics and maternal wards revealed stacks of paper cards stored in boxes, plastic bags, or drawers. Each clinic used its own format, with no shared patient identity. People frequently moved between states, losing their entire medical history in the process. Logbooks replaced digital systems, making errors common and reporting slow. Despite these limitations, smartphone usage was high, and there was openness to digital tools as long as they were simple, affordable, and trustworthy. A strong theme of distrust toward centralized systems emerged, shaped by past experiences of data misuse.

### Where the System Breaks

The health record ecosystem breaks at the point of continuity and trust. Clinics operate in isolation with no interoperability. Health workers hold valuable insight but lack systems to document or share it. Health authorities have the power to respond but receive information too late to act preventively. Patients carry the burden, managing fragile paper records that can be lost, altered, or damaged at any time. This fragmentation weakens clinical decision making, delays outbreak response, increases costs through repeated testing, and leaves communities exposed to preventable risks.

### Naming the Real Challenge

At first, the team believed the challenge was the absence of digital health systems. Through deeper engagement, it became clear that the real issue is identity fragility. People do not simply lack digital records. They lack ownership, portability, and protection of their health identity. Without a trusted way to verify records, digitization alone would only replicate existing failures. The challenge is creating a system where individuals own their health information, clinics can trust its authenticity, and authorities can access accurate data in real time without compromising dignity or consent.

### How We Changed

This realization reshaped the team’s approach. The project shifted from building a technical tool to designing a dignity centered system. The team learned that solutions must reduce stress, not add complexity. They saw how deeply record loss affected mothers, chronic illness patients, and mobile populations. Listening to clinic staff highlighted the emotional toll of duplicated work and isolation. These insights grounded the team in the responsibility to build something understandable, low cost, and rooted in lived reality rather than technological ambition.

### The Direction Forward

HealthChain moves forward with a clear vision of patient owned, verifiable health identities supported by mobile accessible infrastructure. The direction emphasizes trust, simplicity, and shared value. Patients control access to their records. Clinics rely on authentic data without fear of manipulation. Health authorities gain timely insight to detect patterns early and respond proactively. The future HealthChain works toward is one where records are not lost, care is continuous, and communities are resilient because information flows securely, transparently, and with respect for the people it represents.
